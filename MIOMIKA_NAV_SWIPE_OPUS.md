Why ชวนเพื่อน, not แปลภาษา
This is a business decision before a UX decision. Translate is utility — it serves the user once they're in. But the nav is the most viewed real estate in the entire product. Whatever lives there gets a free impression every single screen change, every day, forever. That real estate should serve the highest-leverage problem the business has — and for a solo-founder Bangkok-based product with zero marketing budget, that problem is not "do users find utility" (Miomi solves that). The problem is how do users compound into more users.
Putting Translate in the nav means every Miomika user, every day, sees a free reminder to use Translate. Putting ชวนเพื่อน in the nav means every Miomika user, every day, sees a free reminder to invite a friend. The second one creates more Miomika users. The first one does not.
Concretely: at 1,000 users, if 2% of nav-views/week convert into a share action and 15% of shares become signups, ชวนเพื่อน-in-nav becomes the largest acquisition channel the product has, beating any paid channel a solo founder could afford. Translate has no equivalent flywheel.
There's also a Thai cultural fit. Word-of-mouth, group chats, and LINE sharing are the primary way products spread in Thailand. The nav tab makes sharing a one-tap natural behavior, not a discovery in a settings menu. The cultural framing of "ชวนเพื่อน" (invite a friend) is gentle and social, not transactional — fits Miomi's voice. "อ้างอิง" (referral) would be cold and businesslike. "แชร์" (share) is too generic. "ชวนเพื่อน" reads as a warm action, almost a Miomi prompt herself: "ชวนเพื่อนมาเล่นด้วยกันไหมคะ~"
Translate goes in the canvas (mobile: accessed via home screen secondary action; desktop: rail icon per the previous brief). It deserves a place, just not the nav.
Order reasoning — why เรียน is position 3 (true center)
Position 3 of 5 is mathematical center. The pink raised pill sits dead center of the screen, perfectly balanced. The eye lands there first. The flanking pattern Growth/Learn/Invite creates a meaningful arc: see your progress → take action → share your progress. The "Me" tab at position 5 (rightmost) follows the iOS/Android convention that "self" lives at the far right.
Home at position 1 (leftmost) is the universal anchor — leftmost is "back to start" in every Thai user's mental model from LINE, Shopee, TikTok.

2. BORDER FIX — EXACT CSS
The border is conflicting because it's a single border-top on the nav element, and the raised pill is a child of that nav. The pill clips against the border on both edges (where it exits the nav top, and where its bottom curve re-enters).
Decision: kill the border-top. Replace with a soft-shadow + masked SVG notch.
The shadow approach is more elegant than a border because (a) it's already softer visually, fitting the Miomika tone, and (b) it can be punched through cleanly using a mask, while a border line cannot. This also removes the visual heaviness of a hairline rule cutting across the screen — the nav becomes a float, not a contained section.

How this fixes the conflict

The border line is gone entirely. Replaced with a layered shadow (one diffuse soft shadow + one 40%-opacity hairline shadow that only suggests separation). The result reads as "the nav floats" not "the nav is a contained box with a line on top."
The SVG mask in bottom-nav__bg cuts a 9-unit radius circle at the top-center of the background fill (matches the pill location: position 3 of 5 = horizontal center). The pill now passes through negative space in the nav background, not through a border. There is nothing for it to clip against because there is nothing there.
The soft shadow surrounding the pill (0 8px 24px -4px rgba(219, 39, 119, 0.45)) reads as the pill's own glow, not as a collision with the nav. The mask cutout means even the diffuse white shadow of the nav itself doesn't bleed across the pill base.
The label เรียน sits at bottom: -22px relative to the pill, which positions it inside the nav row where the other four labels live. The 5 labels visually align at a single horizontal baseline despite the pill being elevated. This is critical for the row to read as "5 equal tabs" cognitively, even though one is elevated visually.

The viewBox 100 100 with preserveAspectRatio="none" lets the mask stretch to any nav width without recalculating the SVG. The circle is at cx="50" — exact center of the 5-column grid, where the pill lives.

3. SWIPE ARCHITECTURE
Decision: Framer Motion drag on a horizontal track, with snap-back via dragSnapToOrigin disabled and explicit snap targets. Not CSS scroll-snap.
CSS scroll-snap is rejected because:

It doesn't allow velocity-based "swipe past one screen to the next" gestures — only paginated scrolling
It hijacks the scroll behavior of children with vertical scroll, even with overscroll-behavior set
It doesn't allow the nav indicator to animate proportionally to drag distance (a key affordance for "I'm halfway between screens")
It can't be canceled mid-drag based on velocity thresholds

Framer Motion gives full control. We already use it in the codebase.
Direction mapping
Swipe left → next nav position (right in nav order)
Swipe right → previous nav position (left in nav order)
This matches LINE, Shopee, TikTok feeds, and the iOS pageviewcontroller convention. Universal Thai mobile mental model.
Nav order for swipe purposes (left-to-right):
[0] หน้าหลัก  →  [1] แดชบอร์ด  →  [2] เรียน  →  [3] ชวนเพื่อน  →  [4] ฉัน
Swipe right from แดชบอร์ด (index 1) goes to หน้าหลัก (index 0). Swipe left from แดชบอร์ด goes to เรียน (index 2).
At the boundaries (index 0 and index 4), drag is allowed but with rubber-band resistance (Framer Motion's dragElastic: 0.2). Releasing snaps back to the same screen. This is the "you can't go further" affordance — physical, not blocked. Same pattern as iOS Safari pull-to-refresh edges.
Swipe past เรียน — yes, it feels different
When the user drags past the screen adjacent to เรียน (i.e., crossing from แดชบอร์ด toward เรียน, or from ชวนเพื่อน toward เรียน), the raised pill scales up slightly during the drag as a magnetic affordance.
Specifically: as the user's drag progress approaches เรียน's screen (drag distance crosses 50% of viewport width toward เรียน), the pill in the nav grows from scale 1.0 to scale 1.10 over the drag-progress range 50%→100%, then settles back to 1.0 once the screen is locked in. Coming away from เรียน, the pill scales back down 1.0→0.96 then back to 1.0 as you leave.
This creates a subtle gravitational pull toward the primary action. The user doesn't see a "snap" indicator — they feel it. The pill "wants" them to land on it. After 2-3 swipes through it, this becomes a muscle-memory anchor.
Transition animation
Slide. Not fade. Not scale.

The screen being left translates X from 0 to -100vw (when swiping left to next).
The screen being entered translates X from 100vw to 0.
Both animate in tandem during drag (Framer Motion's x prop tracks finger).
On release, snap is a spring: { type: 'spring', stiffness: 320, damping: 32 }. Stiff enough to feel responsive, damped enough to prevent overshoot.

Velocity threshold for committing to next screen: drag must exceed 30% of viewport width OR velocity must exceed 500 px/s. Below both thresholds, snap back to origin. This matches iOS native paging gesture feel.
Tap navigation (user taps a nav icon) uses the same slide animation, just driven by a programmatic x target rather than finger position. Identical visual outcome — keeps the mental model consistent: every screen change is a slide, regardless of input method.
Fade is wrong because it dissolves spatial context (user loses track of "where am I in the app"). Scale is wrong because it implies depth/modal stacking, not lateral navigation. Slide is the only animation that maps to a horizontal nav row.
Internal horizontal scroll — handled
The conflict: word cards in the conversation, vocabulary bank cards in dashboard, and horizontal achievement scrollers all use internal horizontal scroll. Naive swipe-nav would steal those gestures.
Decision: gesture priority resolves via direction-of-intent detection at drag start.
When a drag begins:

The first 8px of movement is the "intent capture" zone.
If the user's initial X-movement is greater than 1.6× their Y-movement, this is a horizontal swipe.
We then check if the drag's origin point is inside a data-horizontal-scroll-zone element.
If yes → the gesture belongs to that scroller. Nav-level drag is disabled for this gesture.
If no → the gesture belongs to nav. The Framer Motion drag handler activates.

Implementation:
tsx// app/(app)/layout.tsx — wraps all 5 screens
'use client'
import { motion, useMotionValue, animate } from 'framer-motion'
import { useRef, useState } from 'react'
import { usePathname, useRouter } from 'next/navigation'

const SCREENS = ['/home', '/dashboard', '/create', '/invite', '/profile']

export default function AppLayout({ children }: { children: React.ReactNode[] }) {
  const pathname = usePathname()
  const router = useRouter()
  const activeIndex = SCREENS.indexOf(pathname)
  
  const x = useMotionValue(0)
  const [dragEnabled, setDragEnabled] = useState(true)
  const dragStartRef = useRef<{ x: number; y: number } | null>(null)
  
  const handlePointerDown = (e: React.PointerEvent) => {
    dragStartRef.current = { x: e.clientX, y: e.clientY }
    
    // Check if drag origin is inside a horizontal scroll zone
    const target = e.target as HTMLElement
    const inHorizontalScroller = target.closest('[data-horizontal-scroll-zone]')
    setDragEnabled(!inHorizontalScroller)
  }
  
  const commitToScreen = (targetIndex: number) => {
    const clamped = Math.max(0, Math.min(SCREENS.length - 1, targetIndex))
    router.push(SCREENS[clamped])
    animate(x, 0, { type: 'spring', stiffness: 320, damping: 32 })
  }
  
  const handleDragEnd = (_: unknown, info: { offset: { x: number }; velocity: { x: number } }) => {
    const viewportWidth = window.innerWidth
    const distanceThreshold = viewportWidth * 0.3
    const velocityThreshold = 500
    
    const passedDistance = Math.abs(info.offset.x) > distanceThreshold
    const passedVelocity = Math.abs(info.velocity.x) > velocityThreshold
    
    if (passedDistance || passedVelocity) {
      const direction = info.offset.x < 0 ? 1 : -1  // swipe left = next (positive direction)
      commitToScreen(activeIndex + direction)
    } else {
      animate(x, 0, { type: 'spring', stiffness: 320, damping: 32 })
    }
  }
  
  return (
    <div className="relative h-[100svh] overflow-hidden bg-[#FAFAF6]" onPointerDown={handlePointerDown}>
      <motion.div
        className="flex h-full w-[500vw]"
        style={{ x, transform: `translateX(${-activeIndex * 100}vw)` }}
        drag={dragEnabled ? 'x' : false}
        dragConstraints={{ left: 0, right: 0 }}
        dragElastic={0.2}
        dragMomentum={false}
        onDragEnd={handleDragEnd}
      >
        {SCREENS.map((path, i) => (
          <section key={path} className="h-full w-screen flex-shrink-0">
            {i === activeIndex ? children : null /* lazy mount adjacent screens — see note */}
          </section>
        ))}
      </motion.div>
      
      <BottomNav activeIndex={activeIndex} dragX={x} />
    </div>
  )
}

Nav indicator animates during swipe — yes
The active-state styling on the nav tabs animates proportionally to drag distance, not just on commit. This is the affordance that tells the user "you're moving between screens, and here's what you'll land on."
Specifically, three things animate based on dragX:

Active tab color crossfade. As the user drags 30%+ toward an adjacent screen, the color of the current active tab interpolates from #DB2777 (active pink) toward #9A8B73 (muted), and the target tab does the inverse. At 50% drag distance, both tabs are at midtone. At 100% drag distance, the target is fully active.
Pill scale (already covered above). Pill scales 1.0 → 1.10 when drag approaches เรียน's screen, regardless of from which direction.
Subtle bottom underline — a 24px wide pink bar sits 2px below the active tab's icon, and it translateXs smoothly between tab positions as the user drags. This is the spatial indicator. The user sees a pink bar literally sliding from one tab to another in real-time as their finger moves. It snaps to the final position on release.

In BottomNav.tsx:
tsxfunction BottomNav({ activeIndex, dragX }: { activeIndex: number; dragX: MotionValue<number> }) {
  const indicatorX = useTransform(dragX, (x) => {
    const viewportWidth = window.innerWidth
    const tabWidth = viewportWidth / 5
    const dragProgress = -x / viewportWidth  // -1 to +1 typically during drag
    return (activeIndex + dragProgress) * tabWidth + tabWidth / 2 - 12
  })
  
  return (
    <nav className="bottom-nav">
      <div className="bottom-nav__bg" aria-hidden />
      <div className="bottom-nav__tabs">
        {/* tabs */}
      </div>
      <motion.div
        className="absolute bottom-[6px] left-0 h-[2px] w-6 rounded-full bg-[#DB2777]"
        style={{ x: indicatorX }}
      />
    </nav>
  )
}
The underline never sits under เรียน (the raised pill has its own visual weight and doesn't need an underline). When drag direction is toward เรียน, the underline travels into the masked notch area and visually "disappears under" the pill — a nice spatial reinforcement that the pill is the destination.
Edge cases handled

Mid-drag tap on a nav icon: the drag is cancelled, animate(x, 0, ...) snaps back to current screen, then the tap navigates programmatically. No conflict because pointer events on nav tabs are processed in a different layer (pointer-events: auto only on .bottom-nav__tabs).
System back gesture (Android edge swipe): the OS gesture takes priority — the drag handler only fires on touches that begin >24px from screen edges. This prevents conflict with Android navigation gestures.
Adjacent screen prefetching: Next.js handles route prefetching automatically. To avoid jank, render only the active screen by default. Optional optimization: render adjacent screens (activeIndex - 1 and activeIndex + 1) in low-priority hidden containers if you want zero-latency reveal during drag. Skip this optimization until you measure a problem.
